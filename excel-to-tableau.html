The Thousand Facets of Progress:
from Excel to Tableau

Once upon a time, there was a world a long time ago, where writing was a mere priviledge of monks and nobles. Before the 15th century nobody thought there was any need of Gutenberg’s printing invention; but then, in less than half century more than 2 thausand books were printed and reading and writing became viral. A while later, before the 70s, names as Silicon Valley didn’t exist; computers were just heavy and boring machines where to enter anonymous codes. Afterwards, men such as Bill Gates and Steve Jobbs came in the spotlight and computers started to be the friendly and omniscient tools which everybody has at home in different seizes and units. All of a sudden the world was changing again.

This endless process of improvement happened thousands of times in human history; discoveries and revolutions have been on people’s agenda from a very long time, starting from Colombo’s America, passing through Oppenheimer’s atomic bomb to new Google’s electronic sheets: all in the name of progress. An eternal proceeding which won’t never stop until there will be pioneers which, in a beat of wings, will be capable of looking straight into the future’ eyes and capture the essence of what the world needs.


“I see the future” proudly said Christian Chabot when he saw what they were inventing with Tableau. He had spent years analyzing data before studying entrepreneurship at Stanford Business School. Together, Chris, Pat and him formed a company and spun out of Stanford in 2003. With Christian on board as CEO, Tableau rapidly hit one success after another: first customers, multiple awards, international expansion, the first million in revenue and multiple new inventions. “Our goal was and remain to liberate the natural curiosity and creative energy stored in people everywhere; energy which is too often suppressed because it hasn't had a proper channel for activity.”




A Way to Reverse the Process

In 2020 the world will generate 50 times the amount of data as in 2011 and 75 times the number of information sources - as if in 3 years the world population doubled without any increase of homes and infrastructures: a total collapse; so to turn opportunities into reality, people need the power of data at their fingertips and by now, can’t settle for obsolete softwares anymore.
Microsoft house’s softwares are still the best competitors on the market but they have done so many steps forward to approach the simple user that there is a need for a company-sized software able to menage data as brain does with thoughts: faster with new times, easier with shortcuts and mostly, in the smartest way to accomplish the best results.

One of the limitations of exporting to Excel is that as soon as a new transaction or adjustment is posted in the accounting system, the previous reports became out of date and the export routines needed to be run all over again; in other words, formulas need to be checked, report accuracy had to be reviewed and any additional change to the way management wanted to look at the report would also need to be processed.

Often there is an inherent risk that a formula in a particular row or column is updated uncorrectly without capturing the new shape of the dataset. At this point, time will need to be devoted to checking the integrity of formulas before circulating fresh reports to management and this phenomenon, for people which have always to deal with duties and deadlines just before the expiration date, it’s not a good sign of confidence.

Therefore, the longer the formula gets and the more sources it draws from, the harder it will be control and retain accuracy when updating the source data. In a perfect world, the source data shape doesn't change and it’s possible to import the fresh data, but rarely there is enough flexibility to catch all the new information; and it’s not finished yet: the bigger formulas and datasets get, the slower they are to update; Excel then hits critical mass and the Crash is around the corner.

A New easier world

The good news in the value of data and the difficulty finding scientists is that technology is making data easier for mere mortals: a clear example is right here and its name is Tableau.

The Tableau world is where you can easily combine data sets, create calculations that always include new data, reduce human error, automate your reporting tasks, enable self-serve analysis, share and update your reports and connect to live data sources.

Tools like this will free organizations to get more out of their data scientists: mostly those people who used to spend all day identifying the problems in the business now will have all day to solve problems or to develop new strategies; that means much greater synergy, strength and depth across the organization in terms of delivering full integrated solutions and delivering far more information for the management of the business
