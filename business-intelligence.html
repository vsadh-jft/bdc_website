A Historic Perspective

Sporadic use of business intelligence dates back to the 1860s, when technologies were still far from how we know them nowadays; the term was first used by H. P. Luhn in an article entitled “A Business Intelligence System” published in the IBM Research Journal in 1958.

Luhn defined BI as “the ability to apprehend the interrelationships of facts in the best way to guide action towards a desired goal.” Arfterwards, consultant Howard Dresner was credited with first proposing it in 1989 as an umbrella phrase for applying data analysis techniques to support business decision-making processes but a more brilliant formulation could be Forrester Research’s definition that describe BI as “a set of processes, architectures, and technologies which transform raw data into meaningful and useful information.”

Business Intelligence - which has also been defined as "non-statistical statistics" (by Paolo Pasini – teacher at Bocconi University) could be the new fuel of this last high-tech century; the potential benefits of its tools will include accelerating and improving decision-making, optimizing internal business processes, increasing operational efficiency and gaining competitive advantage over business rivals, all by its maxim: “the right information reach the right people at the right time”.

BI systems could be also used to enable more effective strategic, tactical, operational insights and decision-making. Mostly it could help companies too, identifying market trends and spotting business problems that need to be addressed.

Evolution Phases

The history of BI nowadays consists of three phases: BI 1.0, BI 2.0 and BI 3.0

BI 1.0 has its origins in the early 1980s, when Ralph Kimball and Bill Inmon invented the concept of Data Warehouse which had the purpose of storing data from many different sources in a single centralised repository, in a non-volatile form not variable over time.

This process of data consultation in the Data Warehouse has been at the core of the birth of several companies in the early 1990s - such as Business Objects, Microstrategy, Crystal Reports and many others - thus completing the first generation’s scenario of software tools for Business Intelligence.

Subsequently BI 2.0, since the 2000s, was aimed to allow the real-time dynamic query of company data by employees - a browser-based approach, in contrast to query tools which had characterised the previous generation of BI software.

BI 3.0 - which operates at the current stage - like other technologies, has shifted from client-server configurations to the web towards mobile devices, making it extremely easy to use, adding collaborative functionalities following the expansion of the user platform by the whole organization.

It became ideal for social workgroups and instead of generating classic monthly, weekly, or daily reports, it is now able to generate them in real-time on any number of processes.
