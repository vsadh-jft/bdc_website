The Potential Power of Artificial Intelligence

From improving healthcare outcomes to the management of traffic levels in metropolitan areas, passing through more powerful marketing campaigns, text analysis, machine learning and synonym meaning; with a more targeted use, the great amount of data we're gathering could well change all areas of our life.

As Standardisation is naturally evolving into automation, products and services’ personalisation is setting off the next wave of digital. Imagine in five years, being able to target people through the articles they've read or through an ideal lookalike audience, or the possibility to improve the accuracy of their site search based on new kind of customisation; those are only few examples of what AI will be capable to do with the right application.

Personal assistants in our mobile phones, profiling, customisation, cyber protection behind commercial interactions: AI will touch almost every aspect of our lives. Its risks and rewards are topics of discussions in nearly every industry circle. It’s a matter of years before it will be possible to use predictive analytics to figure out what target of customers and prospects will be likely to be in the future, adapting their communication models, making personalization easier than ever before.


Like every other technical revolution, the use of AI technology is becoming more prominent not only because already exists the technology ready to support it, but from a while from now, it’s evidently a concrete idea whose time has come. Clearly computer won’t work alone, the next digital marketers will need to work in tandem with machines to analyze data and make decisions based upon it. No matter how much technology evolves, there will always be the need for a human to oversee it: the combination of the two will be far more powerful than just the sum of its parts.

But concretely speaking, how much potential value is up for grabs in the next future? AI could contribute up to $15.7 trillion to the global economy in 2030, more than the current output of China and India combined. Of this, $6.6 trillions (42%) are likely to come from increased productivity while $9.1 trillion (58%) are likely to come from new consumptionside effects. According to Gartner in 2020, AI will become a positive net job motivator, creating 2.3 million jobs, eliminating only 1.8 million.

Just to mention an example, global GDP in 2030, will be up to 14% higher as a result of the accelerating development and take-up of AI. Its economic impact will be initially driven by productivity gains of companies which will augment their existing labour force with AI technologies and increased consumer demand, resulting from the availability of personalised higher-quality products and services.




How does AI works?

But how actually works Artificial Intelligence? its renowned strength belongs to the added value of Algorithms, whose ability lies in finding unpublished or more subtle reading keys between already known information. The expression is the calque of the Latin Algorithmus, in turn derived from Al-Khwarizmi : the name of an Arab mathematician of the ninth century, considered among the first to theorize the concept.

Instead, In the computer science sphere, with algorithm, we mean a set of instructions that must be executed to reach a certain result or solve a problem. As explained by Carlo Vercellis - scientific director of the Big Data Observatory, Milan Polytechnic - the algorithms “uncover hidden correlations that the human eye can not seize, finding new explicative factors and making predictions about the future”.

The matter is easy, there is simply too much information, moving too quickly for humans to process and understand. We are already too much data rich and information poor and that trend is clearly set to accelerate as the digitalization of industries in any sector. Data Science is the core of Machine Learning - a form of Artificial Intelligence able to collectc Data from clients’ web clicking, matching customer’s demand to achieve goods and services at lower prices. Education, health care, law enforcement and many other sectors will benefit from any kind of Machine Learning applications.

Instead in the Business world, there will be two ways in which Machine Learning assists the analyst. The first is efficiency: the analyst doesn’t have to spend valuable time on basic math. The analyst now has more time to think about business implications and next logical steps. Secondly, it helps him to explore and stay in the flow of his analysis because he no longer has to stop and crunch the numbers.

According to IDC, by 2019, 75% of workers whose daily tasks involve the use of enterprise applications will have access to intelligent personal assistants to augment their skills and expertises while by 2021, far more than 50% of enterprises will be spending more per annum on bots and chatbot creation than traditional mobile app development.

These two foundational changes in the way we use information and technology are tightly coupled, perhaps far more tightly than is readily apparent today. Consumers will be mostly attracted to higher quality and more personalised products and services, but will also have the chance to make better use of their time – think of what you could do if you no longer had to drive yourself to work, for example.
