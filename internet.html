In the last 50 years, man has seen 2 main revolutions which brought tremendous progression to the world around him: firstly, the industrial revolution of the 1960s, where automatation in the industries optimised manufacturing, boosted productivity and also changed the way in which factory work was known.

Secondly, Internet Revolution, in the 1990s that brought industrialisation at a global level, transforming media, communication and retail market through a spotortional augmentation of contents, giving to the world a new growth imput.

These first mobile technologies together with Internet drove the first wave of digital, known as the Internet of People even if back in 2008, there were already more objects connected than persons. Then, with the development of the capability to process massive amounts of data in always less time and innovative ways, the foundations of a new revolution have been founded: The Internet of Things.

The way we interact with technology has dramatically changed in recent years – and is still changing: the cost of connecting is decreasing, broadband Internet is become more widely available and technology costs are going down. All of these factors are creating the Perfect Storm for the IoT, which is becoming an increasingly growing topic of conversation both in the workplace and outside of it. It's a concept that not only has the potential to impact how we work but also how we live.

The IoT – which encompasses smart, connected products worldwide is the major contributing player in this exponential increase in data. According to Gartner, by 2020 the number of IoT devices available to consumers will be more than doubled with 20.4 billion IoT devices online.

Just to mention the smartwatch phonomenon, there were 78.1 million wearables sold in 2015 and the market is expected to grow to 411 million by 2020 with an upgrade of 223%. The edge IoT devices, which will act locally based on data they generate, will take advantage from the Cloud for security, configuration, deployment and management, and according to Forrecaster Research, this explosive growth of IoT is expected exceeding more than $5 billion by year-end 2020, becoming the backbone of future customer value.

On a broader scale, the IoT can be applied to things like transportation networks: Smart Cities which can help us reduce waste, loss and cost improving efficiency for things like energy use, recycling or road cleaning; For example, a connected kitchen could save the food and beverage industry as much as 15% annually. The reality is that the IoT allows for virtually endless opportunities and connections to take place, many of which we can't even think of or fully understand yet.
