Market Strategies: A World Flowing into Business Intelligence

Already in the XVIII century, at the time of Industrial Revolution – the England’s golden years, the quest for productivity and profit’s increase was non-stop: the rhythm of factory work was so intense to wear out a man in few years. During these explosive decades, industries, driven by new kind of automation, process improvement and better technologies, reinvented themselves leading to new productivity gains which provided a dramatic upward push on the bottom line. Unfortunately, unlike the past, today’s profitability can be elusive even when productivity improves. In a capitalistic market as the free occidental one, the problem arises because of competition: prices and profit go down, while labour and resource costs are continually increasing.

Coupled with this factor, standard cost analysis are not sufficient to uncover the whole profitability’s cornerstone; in order to remain profitable and improve margins and earnings, companies must innovate themselves in the cost control and accounting’s areas in order to use the relevant information derived from analysis to refocus business operations.

Activity Based Costing - which is one of the first and best market strategies, provides highly granular raw data that, when fed into a multi-dimensional analysis application, can drive to deep insights and unprecedented opportunities for a full maximisation of profit. Introduced by Robert Kaplan in 1987, ABC seeks to assign costs at the business activity level, because activities, unlike margins, can be managed by shining a light on which of them is generating the most cost, giving to the companies the opportunity to take actions to improve margins. In other words ABC states that are products to consume activities, but are the activities (and not the products) which consume resources; so activities are the cost drivers but they are not necessarily based on the volume of production.

The Age of Multi-Dimensionality

The core of the problem is that without using the right suitable margin to measure profitability, it would mean trying to understand a molecular structure by looking through a magnifying glass. You just can’t see what you need because your ability to make correct decisions is limited.

The ability to do multi-dimensional analysis takes business strategies to a new upper level: with multidimensional tools, it’s not only possible to analyse profitability by customer, product and channel anymore but you can look at the intersections: product segment by channel, customer type by brand and so on with the actual increase of thousands of combinations. Powerful multi-dimensional tools, sometimes referred to OLAP technology (On-Line Analytical Processing), have been around for more than a decade now, and have been constantly improving in terms of functionality and scalability; financial reporting systems that once were limited to five dimensions now routinely incorporate 10 to 15.

From Small to Large Companies: Everyone Loves BI

In very small companies, spreadsheets and other ad hoc tools are often enough to get the job done. But as companies grow, the amount of data decision makers need to understand the growth with new products and services, new markets and opportunities, investments in sales, marketing and other systems to support the expansion.

Companies - as diverse as the airlines, the retailers rather than banks or insurers - routinely collect and analyse an enormous amount of data about the buying and usage habits of their customers. Sophisticated mathematical models of this data creates economically perfect scenarios for business intelligence and predictive capability which enable companies to make the right offer at the right time in the right manner and mostly at the right price.

For a bank with multi-dimensional analysis tools, templates can be provided to allow them to analyse profitability by customer, customer segment and product. In the insurance industry, with powerful analysis tools, it is possible to look at profitability across business segments, product lines and individual products or brands. Instead in the airline companies’ world, key points such as distance travelled, availability on seats, freight and fuel are vital factors for decision-making. The airline route optimization model is based on performing Big Data under multiple heuristic methods over large scale, based on which practical problems are analysed.
